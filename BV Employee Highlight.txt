Growing up, my world was a lot different from what most folks experience today. Imagine a small village, tucked right at the foot of Mount Slamet in Central Java, where electricity was a luxury that only showed up around 1998, and the first black-and-white TVs started popping up around 2000. Colored ones? Those were practically alien technology. Honestly, I never in a million years thought I’d end up doing what I do now, especially when some of my neighbors still give you a blank stare if you mention a laptop.

In that kind of environment, thinking out of the box or even just being a little curious could sometimes feel like you were committing a minor sin. While most kids my age were busy running around rice fields, gathering grass for the cows, or collecting firewood in the forest for cooking, I was the odd one out. Reading a book was looked down upon, and drawing? Oh, that was absolutely forbidden. The elders were pretty strict about Islamic rules concerning drawing and sculpting, so my early creative urges had to find other outlets.

My first real encounter with a working PC happened when I was already in high school. All I knew how to do was mess around with Paint and type some words. English also became a subject then, but honestly, I couldn't see how it would ever be useful. My high school was, let's just say, a relic, focusing on heavy industrial machinery. Why did I choose it? Sounded cool at the time, I suppose. It was all part of my weird, winding experience.

Its bad now, but at that time, this is owesoomeee. i believe those were made in a free software called photopos? i didn't know photoshop existed yet, and even if i do my pc will just explode.
But enough about the ancient past, right? Through all of that, being creative and finding the quickest, most efficient way to solve something has always been a habit of mine. It’s the kind of habit that, sometimes, was seen as destructive to the existing system, a bit too much out of the box for comfort. But that exact habit, that relentless drive for problem solving, is precisely what helped me break free from that cage. Well, that, and the fact that after graduation, I packed my bags and moved to the city at 16, finding work in an industrial complex. That move, that chance, finally allowed me to access all the things that were inaccessible before.

Suddenly, during breaks or after work, I could easily get my hands on a PC. And what did I do with it? I learned to draw digitally, tried my hand at writing short stories, or just mindlessly browsed the internet. And yes, when I say "mindlessly," I mean staring at a screen for a full minute waiting for a 300KB picture to load. Forget about coding, Photoshopping, or designing a website; a Pentium 3 PC couldn't even handle that kind of heavy lifting. But even with those limitations, that time was invaluable. It was my first real experience getting familiar with computers, how they worked, and I started to learn editing. The results, while certainly not masterpieces, absolutely amused me. It felt monumental, a real step in my personal journey of creativity.

After a few months of playing around during and after working hours—and just to be clear, this never affected my actual job; I always got my work done perfectly—I decided to brace myself for what felt like an embarrassing challenge. I went around the local market, offering to make printed banners for shops, for free. Surprisingly, most of them accepted! And because of that, I managed to save up enough to buy my very first secondhand PC: a Pentium 4! Yes, a Pentium 4! It only lasted less than a year, but it was enough to help me learn the basics of Photoshop and CorelDRAW. That little victory made me a bit overconfident, and I was soon humbled by the sheer talent and competition in the city. Another valuable experience, I suppose.

Anyway, I don't really have any specific, grand lesson here, or some profound revelation to share. This is mostly just me babbling about how genuinely amazed I am to be who I am now. I learned everything on the way, little by little, with whatever I could afford and stumble upon. I only learned about websites, for example, at the tail end of the COVID era. It was actually a challenge from my boss, who, bless his heart, didn't want to pay extra to hire a new webmaster. At that time, I was already juggling roles as a photographer, videographer, graphic designer, and social media person, among other things. Don't even ask how much I was paid; I was far too innocent back then i still do tho, ha! And English? That's the one thing I absolutely never expected to understand. I couldn't even string together a basic grammatically correct sentence when I first found myself stranded in Bali in 2017. But that, my friends, is a story for later.

This was made before AI is a thing, people nowadays said it was AI which is understandable. but whatever, doing it manualy still feels fun.

At the very least, now i can create more freely with what i have learned, lacking? of course, that just means that there are lot more things i will have to learn, isn't that exciting?
My point in all this rambling, this sharing of my own out of the box experience, is simply this: don't overthink things. As long as you don't stop learning and you don't limit your own possibilities, pretty much everything is doable. It doesn't matter how far behind you started, or how minimal your access might be to whatever support you think you need to become what you want to be. Just take it little by little. That consistent drive for problem solving, combined with a bit of creativity, can really take you places you never imagined.
